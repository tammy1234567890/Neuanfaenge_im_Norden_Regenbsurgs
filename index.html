NEW BEGINNINGS IN THE NORTH OF REGENSBURG

My semester project is very close to my heart. Since my mother began working for the Nord district project in Regensburg in 2015, I have followed her work and heard many of her stories. The district project is part of the Office for Youth and Family of the City of Regensburg. My mother works with people in a wide range of life situations, often those affected by poverty, frequently refugees, and mostly children with migrant backgrounds. With a strong focus on child welfare, she helps stabilize family situations. She provides support and guidance through the bureaucratic “jungle,” as well as in everyday life and parenting matters. Through this work, she witnesses first-hand the invisible realities of life in northern Regensburg: housing that is far too small, often affected by mould; isolated lives, particularly of women and their young children; struggles for education and participation; language barriers; and precarious employment. These conditions are part of daily life for many families and have long-term consequences. My mother and her colleagues receive an immense amount of gratitude every day. For a long time now, we have both been thinking about how to make visible those who are otherwise pushed to the margins of society, while also drawing attention to the important work of the Nord district project. At the same time, these issues have been consistently ignored by local politics in Regensburg for years.

FAMILY ON SCHLESIER STRASSE The family of nine had to flee their home in the Sinjar region of northern Iraq during the 2014 genocide against the Yazidis. They arrived in Regensburg in 2015, where they initially lived in municipal emergency accommodation. In 2016, they found a half of a duplex house and have been living there ever since. Since then, the family’s situation has stabilized. Five of the children are currently in vocational training or have already completed it. The eldest son is attending master craftsman school to become a certified automotive master mechanic. After a period of precarious employment and subsequent exhaustion, the father has also managed to secure stable employment again. One son is now married, and the household has grown to ten people living in the same house. Due to the poor condition of the building, the housing situation remains unsatisfactory. The family has been supported by the Nord district project since 2015, making them one of the longest-accompanied families in the program.

FAMILY AT FLACHLBERG The mother and her son first came to the district project for counseling in 2022, following the tragic death of her husband and the boy’s father. During the first year, the family required very intensive support. Many unfamiliar and unclear situations had to be navigated, and building trust played a key role, as the mother was not yet familiar with the German system. One important issue was the son’s transition from kindergarten to primary school. For the mother, the most important thing has always been her son’s well-being. In the meantime, she has successfully re-entered the workforce and started a permanent job in January 2025. The mother originally comes from Algeria and has held German citizenship since 2023. Her son was born in Regensburg.

ANOTHER FAMILY ON SCHLESIER STRASSE The family, with four children in total, is currently living in very cramped conditions: five people share a 47-square-meter two-room apartment. The eldest daughter is currently living with her Italian father in Belgium. The mother originally comes from Bulgaria, while the father is from Romania. Both parents lived and worked for many years in southern Italy, including in the tourism sector. The family moved to Regensburg several years ago, where their three younger children were born. The father now works installing solar panels. The family has been supported by the district project since 2021.
